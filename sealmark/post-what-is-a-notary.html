What Is a Notary Public and When Do You Need One in New York?

Notary Basics · 6 min read

Most people go their whole life without thinking about a notary. Then one Tuesday afternoon a form crosses their desk, a bank teller mentions "sign in the presence of a notary," or a hospital hands them a healthcare proxy and asks if they can get it witnessed by the end of the week.

Suddenly the word matters. This guide is for that Tuesday.

The Simple Definition

A notary public is a person the State of New York has authorized to serve as an official, impartial witness for certain kinds of document signings. Their job is to confirm three things: that the person signing is who they say they are, that the person understands what they are signing, and that the person is signing willingly.

When those three boxes are checked, the notary applies a seal or stamp and signs the document themselves. That seal is what allows courts, banks, embassies, title companies, and government agencies to trust the document later, sometimes years later.

What a Notary Actually Does

In New York, the most common notarial acts are:

  • Acknowledgments. The signer appears before the notary and confirms that they signed the document voluntarily. Common for deeds, contracts, powers of attorney.
  • Jurats. The signer swears (or affirms) under oath that the contents of the document are true. Common for affidavits, sworn statements, and some court filings.
  • Copy certifications (limited in New York, but available for certain document types).
  • Oaths and affirmations for people about to give testimony or take office.

What a Notary Cannot Do

This part matters just as much as the rest.

  • A notary cannot give legal advice. Not about your document, not about your situation, not about what you should sign.
  • A notary cannot draft or fill in your document for you.
  • A notary cannot certify a copy of a birth certificate, marriage certificate, or other vital records issued by the state. Those need certified copies from the issuing office.
  • A notary cannot notarize a signature they didn't personally witness. The signer must appear before them.

If someone tells you they can notarize a document you already signed at home, or that they can help you write your will, walk away. That is not how New York notaries operate.

When Do You Need a Notary in New York?

You'll need a notary more often than you'd expect. Common moments include:

  • Signing a power of attorney or healthcare proxy
  • Buying or selling a home (deeds, mortgages, and many closing documents)
  • Certain wills, trusts, and estate documents
  • Vehicle title transfers and DMV forms
  • Business filings, corporate resolutions, LLC operating agreements
  • Affidavits for immigration, court proceedings, or insurance claims
  • Documents traveling abroad that will need an apostille
  • Some school and university forms
  • Loan signings for refinances, purchases, and HELOCs

What to Bring

A notary appointment goes smoothly when you arrive with:

  • A current, government-issued photo ID (driver's license, passport, state ID, or military ID)
  • The complete document, unsigned. Do not sign it before the notary sees it.
  • Every required signer, if the document needs more than one signature
  • Any specific instructions from the receiving party (a title company, embassy, employer)
The seal is small. What it stands for is not.

Notarization is one of those quiet civic acts. It doesn't make headlines. But it is what allows strangers, decades apart, to trust that a signature meant what it said. That is worth doing carefully.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a notary charge in New York?

New York sets a maximum statutory fee of $2.00 per notarized signature for traditional in-person notarization. Mobile notaries also charge a separate travel or convenience fee for coming to your location, which is quoted and agreed before the appointment. Remote Online Notarization and specialized services like loan signings are priced differently.

Do I need to sign the document before I see the notary?

No. In almost all cases you should sign the document in the notary's presence, not beforehand. The notary's job is to witness your signature. If you have already signed, the notary generally cannot notarize it and you may need to re-sign a fresh copy.

What identification do I need for a notary in New York?

You need a current, government-issued photo ID such as a driver's license, passport, state ID, or military ID. The name on your ID should reasonably match the name on the document being signed.

Can a notary give me legal advice about my document?

No. A notary public is not a lawyer and cannot give legal advice, explain what your document means, or tell you what to sign. For legal guidance, you should consult a licensed New York attorney.
